Cyril’s claim that “the return to Nature would come to mean simply the advance to a great personality” could be construed in two ways. Either, it may constitute an identification with a Pantheistic Oversoul that permeates all “persons”—drawing upon Emerson and earlier Romantic contentions of nature. Or, it may be a view of Nature as “abolishing” individuality—the erasure of individuality and subjective experience through the connection or “return to” an all-encompassing Absolute connection to “Nature,” creating a singular “objective” experience. These positions are not mutually exclusive, and indeed many scholars have read pantheism and Romantic conceptions of nature as an subsumption of individuality by the impersonal. Relatedly, Hilda Schiff’s influential reading of the essay argues that Wilde offers a conception of the artist-nature relationship in which, in deference to nature as the ideal, nature not only fails to “unite” with the individual, but “abolishes” the artist by “abstracting” the artist (“Nature and Art in Oscar Wilde’s ‘The Decay of Lying’ 87). 64. Thomas Carlyle, the Scottish Essayists and historian, was also a translator of many German Romantic works. His translations, as well as Sartor Resartus, were markedly influential on the American Transcendentalist movement, figure headed by Emerson, but which also included writers like Margaret Fuller and Henry David Thoreau. In fact, Edgar Allan Poe, alluded to by Wilde in works like “The Harlot’s House” and a key point of reference for British Decadent writers (and also, like Wilde, a habitual “self-plagiarist), continually criticized the New England Transcendentalists writers for the overt Carlylean style and conceits. For example, in 1842, Poe derided Emerson’s prose for being obscurest and unintelligible: “Quintilian mentions a pedant who taught obscurity, and who once said to a pupil “this is excellent, for I do not understand it myself.” How the good man would have chuckled over Mr. E.! His present rôle seems to be the out-Carlyling Carlyle. Lycophron Tenebrosus is a fool to him” (“AN APPENDIX OF AUTOGRAPHS”).
