Mr Ruskin once described the characters in George Eliot's novels as being like 'the sweepings of a Pentonville omnibus',Fiction – Fair and Foul V’, Nineteenth Century, 10 (1881), 521 but M. Zola's characters are much worse. They have their dreary vices, and their drearier virtues. The record of their lives is absolutely without interest. Who cares what happens to them? In literature we require distinction, charm, beauty, and imaginative power. We dont want to be harrowed and disgusted with an account of the doings of the lower orders. M. Alphonse Daudet is better. He has espirit , a light touch, and an amusing style. But he has lately committed literary suicide. Noonebody can possibly care for Delobelle with his 'Il faut lutter pour l'art Je n'ai pas le droit de renouncer,' or for Valmajour with his eternal refrain about the nightingale, or for the poet in 'Jack' with his 'mots cruels,'' now that we have learned from 'Vingt Ans de ma Vie littéraire' that these characters were directly copied directly taken from life. To me they seem to have suddenly lost all their vitality, all the few qualities that they ever possessed. The only real people are the people that who never existed; and if a novelist is base enough to go directly to life for his personages, he should at least pretend that they are creations, and not boast of them as copies.
